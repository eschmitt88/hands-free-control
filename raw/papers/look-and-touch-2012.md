**Title:** Look & Touch: Gaze-supported Target Acquisition

**Authors:** Sophie Stellmach, Raimund Dachselt (Interactive Media Lab, Technische Universitat Dresden).

**Year:** 2012

**Venue:** Proceedings of the SIGCHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems (CHI '12), pp. 2981-2990. ACM. DOI 10.1145/2207676.2208709.

**Abstract (paraphrased/quoted from search-captured text — verify against source before quoting verbatim):**
While eye tracking has high potential for fast selection tasks, it is often regarded as error-prone and unnatural, especially for gaze-only interaction. To improve on that, the authors propose gaze-supported interaction as a more natural and effective way of combining a user's gaze with touch input from a handheld device. They contribute a set of novel and practical gaze-supported selection techniques for distant displays, designed according to the principle "gaze suggests, touch confirms." These include an enhanced gaze-directed cursor, local zoom lenses, and more elaborate techniques using manual fine-positioning of the cursor via touch. In a comprehensive user study with 24 participants, they investigated the potential of these techniques across different target sizes and distances.

**Contributions / claims (as captured):**
- The "gaze suggests, touch confirms" design principle for gaze-supported target acquisition on distant displays.
- A family of techniques: enhanced gaze-directed cursor, local zoom lenses, and touch-based manual fine positioning.
- User study (N=24) across target sizes and distances.

**Method (as captured):** Gaze provides coarse cursor positioning on a remote/distant display; a handheld touch device refines position (zoom lens / manual offset) and confirms selection — decoupling the "where" (gaze, fast but imprecise) from the "commit" (touch, precise and deliberate) to avoid the Midas-touch problem.

**Results:** Comparative study of techniques across target sizes/distances; detailed numeric outcomes behind the paywall.
